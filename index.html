My Academic Journey

I went to California High School in the East Bay Area of Northern California. During my junior year, I had the incredible privilege to study abroad in France at the American School of Grenoble. Upon return to Cal High for my final year after an immersive year as an exchange student, I had set my eyes upon UC Irvine's undergraduate program in Biological Sciences.
My initial exposure to the physical sciences began with the General Chemistry sequence, but it all changed when I began Calculus I. I came in with a shaky foundation of precalculus and trigonometry. During the length of the first two weeks of Calculus I, I felt I had reached my mental limit spending the first few hours of each morning trying to wrap my head around the subject and feeling completely overwhelemed and inadequate. Through the toil, however, there was a growing desire towards absorbing the material. After deep work and focus, I had scored a 39/40 on the first midterm examination exactly five weeks from the start of the course. A couple hours later, my score changed to 40/40, the answer key had a mistake. I was delighted, but it did not come to me as a surprise. This was the first of many times that I would enter the time warp– the intellectual black-hole where I would meet my limit, and through diligence, optimism, and persistence of thinking I would grow. A wave of content and a sense of accomplishment. All it took was five weeks to go from learning basic trigonometric functions to applying the chain rule for to derivatives.
During my freshman year, I was exposed to the physical sciences. Courses in general chemistry and calculus. I musterd up the courage to make a switch into the Applied Physics major. Shortly thereafter, I declared a double major in mathematics and enrolled in core computer science courses. During this time, I had the oppurtunity and privilege to join Dr. Howard Lee's Nanophotonics Laboratory and work under my graduate student mentor David Dang to employ novel machine learning models to tackle physical problems.
